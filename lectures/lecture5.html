1. Risk Treatment Overview

What is Risk Treatment?

Risk treatment is the process of selecting and applying measures to manage information security risks after they have been identified and assessed. In ISO 27001, the goal is to reduce risks to an acceptable level and decide how each risk will be handled.

Four Risk Treatment Options (ISO 27001)

There are four risk treatment options available in an ISO 27001 risk treatment plan:

ISO 27001 Risk Treatment Options

✅

Accept

Accept the risk when treatment cost exceeds the benefit

🚫

Avoid

Eliminate the activity that creates the risk

🔄

Share

Transfer part of the risk to a third party

📉

Reduce

Implement controls to lower likelihood and/or impact

2. Accept the Risk (Risk Acceptance)

What is Risk Acceptance?

Accepting the risk means an organization decides to take no further action to reduce a specific risk and is willing to live with its potential impact.

This option is usually chosen when:

  • The cost of controls is higher than the potential damage
  • The impact and likelihood are low
  • The risk is unavoidable or minimal
  • Management agrees the risk is within the organization's risk appetite

Key Points

  • No new security controls are implemented
  • The risk is formally approved by management
  • The risk must be documented and monitored regularly

Example: Risk Acceptance

A company may accept the risk of a short website outage because the cost of building a fully redundant global system is too high compared to the small business impact.

3. Avoid the Risk (Risk Avoidance)

What is Risk Avoidance?

Avoiding the risk means an organization decides to eliminate the activity, system, or process that creates the risk, so the risk no longer exists.

This option is chosen when:

  • The risk is too high or unacceptable
  • The risk cannot be reduced to an acceptable level
  • The potential impact is severe (e.g., legal, safety, or major financial damage)

Key Points

  • The risky activity is stopped, removed, or not started
  • The risk is completely eliminated, not just reduced
  • It often involves changing business processes, technology, or strategy
  • Management approval is required because it may affect operations or opportunities

Example: Risk Avoidance

A company may avoid the risk of data leakage from personal USB drives by completely banning USB storage devices on company computers.

4. Share the Risk (Risk Transfer)

What is Risk Sharing?

Sharing the risk means an organization passes part of the risk to another party, while usually still keeping some responsibility. The risk is not removed, but its financial or operational impact is reduced by involving a third party.

This option is chosen when:

  • The organization cannot fully control or reduce the risk on its own
  • Another party is better equipped to manage the risk
  • It is more cost-effective to transfer part of the risk

Key Points

  • Part of the risk is transferred, not eliminated
  • Common methods: insurance, outsourcing, cloud services, contracts, partnerships
  • Legal agreements define responsibilities and liabilities
  • The organization must still monitor the risk and the provider

Examples: Risk Sharing

  • Buying cyber insurance to cover financial losses from data breaches
  • Outsourcing data storage to a secure cloud provider
  • Using a third-party security company to manage monitoring

5. Reduce the Risk (Risk Reduction / Mitigation)

What is Risk Reduction?

Reducing the risk means an organization takes actions and implements controls to lower the likelihood and/or the impact of a risk. This is the most commonly used risk treatment option in information security.

This option is chosen when:

  • The risk is significant but manageable
  • It is possible to apply technical, administrative, or physical controls
  • The organization wants to continue the activity, but in a safer way

Key Points

  • The risk is not removed, but minimized
  • Focuses on reducing: Likelihood (preventive controls) and Impact (corrective and recovery controls)
  • Requires continuous monitoring and improvement

Types of Controls Used

🛡️

Preventive Controls (Before an Incident)

  • Security awareness training
  • Access controls and authentication
  • Firewalls, antivirus, IDS/IPS
🔧

Corrective / Recovery Controls (After an Incident)

  • Data backups
  • Incident response procedures
  • Disaster recovery and business continuity plans

6. Two Steps to Reduce Risk

There are two steps to reduce risk as part of the risk treatment plan:

Step 1: Preventive Methods

  • Human resources and staff training
  • Legislation compliance
  • Quality control measures
  • Auditing
  • Regular maintenance
  • Security systems installation

Step 2: Risk Reduction Procedures

  • Data backups
  • Emergency procedures
  • Minimize exposure to highest-rated risks

7. Development of Action Plan

What is a Treatment Plan?

A treatment plan (or risk treatment plan) is a formal document that explains how an organization will implement its chosen risk treatment options (accept, avoid, share, reduce). It turns risk decisions into practical actions.

Purpose of a Treatment Plan

A treatment plan is used to:

  • Describe what actions will be taken to treat each risk
  • Assign who is responsible
  • Define deadlines and priorities
  • Identify resources (budget, people, tools)
  • Explain how effectiveness will be measured and monitored

What a Treatment Plan Contains

A good risk treatment plan typically includes:

# Component Description
1 Identified risks The risks that were found during assessment
2 Chosen treatment option Accept, avoid, share, or reduce
3 Security controls / actions Specific controls to be implemented
4 Responsible persons or teams Who will carry out the actions
5 Timeframes and milestones When actions must be completed
6 Required resources Budget, tools, and people needed
7 Performance measures How effectiveness will be monitored

Example: Treatment Plan for Data Loss

If the risk is data loss, the treatment plan may include:

  • Action: Implement automated backups
  • Responsible: IT Manager
  • Deadline: 30 days
  • Resources: Backup software + storage
  • Monitoring: Monthly recovery tests

8. Creating a Risk Treatment Plan

Key steps required to create a risk treatment plan:

Steps to Create a Risk Treatment Plan

1

Conduct Risk Assessment

Identify and prioritize information security risks

2

Develop Mitigation Strategy

Decide how each risk will be handled (accept, avoid, share, or reduce)

3

Implement Controls

Apply technical, administrative, or physical controls

4

Monitor and Review

Continuously check effectiveness and improve when needed

Statement of Applicability (SoA)

The Statement of Applicability should be completed before creating the risk treatment plan, as it identifies which ISO 27001 controls are relevant to your organization.

9. Identification of Residual Risks

What is Residual Risk?

Residual risk is the risk that remains after risk treatment options have been selected and controls have been implemented. Even when risks are reduced, avoided, or shared, it is almost impossible to eliminate all risk completely.

Why Residual Risks Must Be Identified

Organizations must identify residual risks in order to:

  • Know what level of risk still exists
  • Decide whether the remaining risk is acceptable
  • Ensure management awareness and approval
  • Meet ISO 27001 requirements

Residual Risks Must Always Be:

  • Documented
  • Approved by management
  • Regularly reviewed

Example: Residual Risk

After installing firewalls and MFA, the risk of hacking is reduced from high to low, but a low risk of advanced cyberattacks still remains. This remaining risk is the residual risk, and it must be formally accepted and monitored.

10. Most Effective Risk Treatment Strategy

Which Strategy is Best?

The answer depends on the types of risk that you are dealing with as an organization and the associated risk levels.

Key Considerations

  • A cost-benefit analysis is vital in picking the right strategy, whether you choose to avoid or accept the risk
  • Key stakeholders must be involved in choosing the best treatment option
  • Develop realistic objectives to match your risk assessment process

Who are Stakeholders?

Stakeholders are individuals, groups, or organizations that have an interest in, are affected by, or can influence an organization's activities, decisions, or outcomes.

Summary and Key Takeaways

What We've Learned

  1. Risk Treatment is the process of selecting and applying measures to manage information security risks. ISO 27001 provides four treatment options: Accept, Avoid, Share, and Reduce.
  2. Risk Acceptance means living with the risk when treatment cost exceeds the benefit. It requires formal management approval and documentation.
  3. Risk Avoidance eliminates the activity causing the risk entirely. Used when risk is too high and cannot be adequately controlled.
  4. Risk Sharing (Transfer) passes part of the risk to a third party through insurance, outsourcing, or cloud services.
  5. Risk Reduction (Mitigation) is the most common option. It uses preventive and corrective controls to lower likelihood and impact.
  6. Treatment Plans are formal documents that turn risk decisions into practical, measurable actions with assigned responsibilities and timelines.
  7. Residual Risk always remains after treatment and must be documented, approved, and monitored.
