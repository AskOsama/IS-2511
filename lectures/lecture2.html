1. Overview

What is Security Risk Assessment?

Security Risk Assessment is a systematic process of identifying, analyzing, and evaluating security risks to an organization's information assets. It helps organizations make informed decisions about how to protect their assets effectively.

Key Questions Risk Assessment Answers

  • What assets need protection? - Identifying critical information assets
  • What threats exist? - Understanding potential sources of harm
  • What vulnerabilities are present? - Identifying weaknesses that could be exploited
  • What are the risks? - Combining likelihood and impact
  • What controls should be implemented? - Selecting appropriate countermeasures

Consequences of Not Doing Risk Assessment

  • Resources wasted on protecting low-value assets
  • Critical assets left unprotected
  • Compliance failures and legal penalties
  • Unexpected security incidents with severe impact
  • Loss of customer trust and reputation damage

Why Choose an Approach? Organizations must choose their risk assessment approach based on their size, resources, regulatory requirements, and risk tolerance. There is no one-size-fits-all solution.

2. Baseline Approach

What is the Baseline Approach?

The Baseline Approach involves implementing a standard set of security controls based on industry best practices, without conducting a detailed risk analysis. It uses pre-defined security standards and checklists.

How It Works

Organizations select a recognized security standard or framework and implement its recommended controls:

  • ISO 27002: Code of practice with 93 security controls
  • NIST Cybersecurity Framework: US government standard with 5 functions
  • CIS Controls: Prioritized list of 18 critical security controls
  • Industry-specific standards: PCI DSS (payment cards), HIPAA (healthcare)

Typical Baseline Controls

  • Password policies: Minimum length, complexity requirements, expiration
  • Multi-factor authentication (MFA): Something you know + something you have
  • Firewalls: Network perimeter protection
  • Regular patching: Keeping systems updated
  • Backup procedures: Regular data backups with testing
  • Antivirus/Antimalware: Endpoint protection
  • Access controls: Role-based access management

Advantages: Easy to implement, low cost, fast deployment, provides a good starting point for organizations new to security, and ensures compliance with industry standards.

Limitations

  • Not tailored to specific organizational needs
  • May result in over-securing low-risk areas (wasting resources)
  • May under-secure high-risk areas (leaving gaps)
  • Does not consider unique threats or business context
  • "One size fits all" approach may not fit well

Example: Small Retail Store

A small retail store implements PCI DSS baseline controls for handling credit card data. They install a firewall, encrypt card data, and use strong passwords. However, they may not need the same level of physical security controls as a data center, and they may have unique risks related to point-of-sale terminals that the baseline doesn't fully address.

3. Informal Approach

What is the Informal Approach?

The Informal Approach relies on the knowledge, experience, and judgment of security experts or experienced staff to identify and assess risks without following a formal methodology.

How It Works

Security decisions are made based on:

  • Expert judgment: Drawing on years of security experience
  • Interviews: Talking with staff about their concerns and observations
  • Workshops: Group discussions to brainstorm risks
  • Intuition: "Gut feeling" about what might go wrong
  • Past incidents: Learning from previous security events

Typical Activities

  • Walkthroughs of facilities and systems
  • Informal conversations with department heads
  • Review of recent security incidents
  • Quick security audits based on experience
  • Brainstorming sessions with IT staff

Advantages: Fast to perform, flexible and adaptable, can be organization-specific, low cost, good for identifying obvious risks quickly, and leverages deep organizational knowledge.

Limitations

  • Highly subjective - depends on the expert's knowledge
  • Results vary between assessors
  • May miss risks outside the expert's experience
  • Difficult to justify decisions to auditors or management
  • No documentation or audit trail
  • Expert bias can lead to blind spots

Example: IT Manager Assessment

An experienced IT manager walks through the office and notices that server room access is not restricted. Based on past experience at other companies, they recommend installing a card access system. While this addresses a real risk, they might miss other risks like insider threats or social engineering because they haven't systematically analyzed all possible threats.

4. Formal Approach (Detailed Risk Analysis)

What is the Formal Approach?

The Formal Approach (also called Detailed Risk Analysis) is a structured, documented process that systematically identifies, analyzes, and evaluates all security risks. It uses a defined methodology to calculate risk scores.

How It Works

The formal approach follows a structured process:

  1. Asset Identification: Catalog all information assets
  2. Asset Classification: Determine value and criticality
  3. Threat Identification: List all potential threats
  4. Vulnerability Assessment: Identify weaknesses
  5. Likelihood Assessment: Estimate probability of threats
  6. Impact Assessment: Estimate consequences if threats materialize
  7. Risk Calculation: Combine likelihood and impact
  8. Risk Prioritization: Rank risks for treatment

Risk Scoring Formula

Risk is calculated by multiplying Likelihood by Impact:

Risk Score = Likelihood (L) × Impact (I)

Both Likelihood and Impact are typically rated on a scale of 1-5:

Rating Likelihood Description Impact Description
1 - Very Low Rare, unlikely to occur Negligible impact
2 - Low Possible but not expected Minor impact, easily recovered
3 - Medium Could occur occasionally Moderate impact, some disruption
4 - High Likely to occur Significant impact, major disruption
5 - Very High Almost certain to occur Catastrophic, business-threatening

Risk Score Ranges

The resulting risk scores (1-25) are categorized into risk levels:

Score Range Risk Level Action Required
1-5 LOW Accept or monitor
6-12 MEDIUM Implement controls when feasible
15-25 HIGH Immediate action required

5×5 Risk Assessment Matrix

Green = Low Risk (1-5) | Yellow = Medium Risk (6-12) | Red = High Risk (15-25)

Advantages: Accurate and comprehensive, provides defensible decisions, creates documentation for compliance, identifies all risks systematically, and supports informed resource allocation.

Limitations

  • Time-consuming to perform thoroughly
  • Expensive - requires skilled resources
  • Requires trained risk assessment experts
  • Can be overwhelming for small organizations
  • May produce analysis paralysis
  • Needs regular updates as threats change

5. Combined Approach

What is the Combined Approach?

The Combined Approach blends elements of Baseline, Informal, and Formal approaches to create a practical, efficient risk assessment process that balances thoroughness with resource constraints.

How It Works - The 3-Stage Process

Combined Approach Flow

1

Baseline

Apply standard controls first

2

Informal

Expert review for gaps

3

Formal

Deep analysis for high-risk areas

Stage 1: Baseline (Foundation)

Start by implementing standard industry controls:

  • Select appropriate security framework (ISO 27002, NIST, CIS)
  • Implement all applicable baseline controls
  • This provides immediate protection for common threats
  • Achieves "minimum acceptable security level"

Stage 2: Informal (Gap Analysis)

Use expert judgment to identify gaps and organization-specific risks:

  • Conduct interviews with key stakeholders
  • Review unique business processes and assets
  • Identify risks not covered by baseline
  • Prioritize areas requiring deeper analysis

Stage 3: Formal (Deep Dive)

Apply detailed risk analysis only to high-value or high-risk areas:

  • Conduct full risk assessment for critical assets
  • Calculate risk scores for prioritized threats
  • Document findings for compliance requirements
  • Justify additional security investments

Why the Combined Approach is Recommended: It balances cost, speed, and accuracy. Organizations get immediate protection from baseline controls, leverage expert knowledge for quick wins, and invest formal analysis resources only where they matter most.

Limitations

  • May initially miss some risks in areas not prioritized for formal analysis
  • Requires coordination between different assessment activities
  • Still needs skilled resources for informal and formal stages
  • May be complex to manage for very small teams

Example: Medium-Sized Hospital

A hospital uses the combined approach:

  1. Baseline: Implements HIPAA-required controls across all systems
  2. Informal: Security officer identifies unique risks in their telemedicine platform
  3. Formal: Conducts detailed risk analysis only for the patient records database and telemedicine system, which contain the most sensitive data

This approach protects all areas while focusing detailed analysis on the most critical systems.

6. Comparison of Approaches

The following table compares all four risk assessment approaches across key features:

Feature Baseline Informal Formal Combined
Cost Low Low High Medium
Time Required Fast Fast Slow Moderate
Expertise Needed Low High High Medium-High
Accuracy Low-Medium Medium High High
Tailored to Organization No Yes Yes Yes
Documentation Checklist only Minimal Comprehensive Comprehensive
Compliance Support Good Poor Excellent Excellent
Best For Small orgs, quick start Experienced teams Regulated industries Most organizations

Recommendation: For most organizations, the Combined Approach offers the best balance of protection, cost, and compliance. Start with baseline controls, use expert judgment to identify unique risks, and apply formal analysis where it matters most.

Summary and Key Takeaways

What We've Learned

  1. Security Risk Assessment is essential for protecting information assets effectively and allocating security resources wisely.
  2. The Baseline Approach uses standard controls from frameworks like ISO 27002, NIST, and CIS Controls. It's fast and cheap but not tailored to specific needs.
  3. The Informal Approach relies on expert judgment and experience. It's flexible but subjective and may miss risks.
  4. The Formal Approach (Detailed Risk Analysis) uses systematic methods to calculate risk scores (L × I). It's accurate but time-consuming and expensive.
  5. Risk Scoring uses a 5×5 matrix where scores 1-5 are Low, 6-12 are Medium, and 15-25 are High risk.
  6. The Combined Approach is recommended for most organizations - start with baseline, add expert review, and apply formal analysis to critical areas.
  7. Approach Selection depends on organization size, resources, regulatory requirements, and risk tolerance.
  8. No approach is perfect - each has advantages and limitations that must be considered.
