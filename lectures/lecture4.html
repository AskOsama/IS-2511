1. What is Risk Treatment?

What is Risk Treatment?

Risk treatment is the process of selecting and implementing measures to modify risk. After risks have been identified, analyzed, and evaluated through risk assessment, the organization must decide how to handle each risk based on its level and the cost of treatment.

The Treatment Decision

The decision to treat a risk depends on the balance between the risk level and the cost of treatment:

Risk Level vs. Cost of Treatment

  • Extreme risk + Low treatment cost: Implement treatment immediately
  • High risk + Moderate treatment cost: Treatment is generally justified
  • Medium risk + Moderate treatment cost: Judgment needed — weigh costs vs. benefits
  • Low risk + High treatment cost: Uneconomic to treat — accept the risk

Key Principle: Risk treatment is not about eliminating all risks — that is impossible and impractical. The goal is to reduce risks to an acceptable level within the organization's risk appetite, while making efficient use of available resources.

2. Risk Treatment Alternatives

There are five main alternatives for treating identified risks. The choice depends on the nature of the risk, its level, and the organization's circumstances:

Five Risk Treatment Alternatives

✅

Accept

Accept the risk when treatment cost exceeds the benefit

🚫

Avoid

Do not proceed with the activity causing the risk

🔄

Transfer

Shift the risk to a third party (insurance, outsourcing)

📉

Reduce Consequence

Modify asset usage to reduce impact if risk occurs

🛡️

Reduce Likelihood

Implement controls to make the risk less likely to occur

1. Risk Acceptance

Accept the risk — perhaps because of the excessive cost of risk treatment. The organization acknowledges the risk exists but decides not to take action.

  • When to use: The cost of treatment exceeds the potential loss, or the risk level is low
  • Requirement: Must be a conscious, documented decision by management

Example: Risk Acceptance

A company's older printer has a known vulnerability, but it is on an isolated network segment, only prints non-sensitive documents, and will be replaced in 3 months. The cost of patching exceeds the minimal risk, so management formally accepts the risk.

2. Risk Avoidance

Do not proceed with the activity that causes the risk. This eliminates the risk entirely but also means losing the convenience or benefit of the activity.

  • When to use: The risk is too high and no adequate controls can reduce it
  • Trade-off: Loss of business opportunity or functionality

Example: Risk Avoidance

A company decides not to offer public Wi-Fi in its offices because the risk of network intrusion through an open wireless network is too high and the security costs to properly protect it are not justified by the business benefit.

3. Risk Transfer

Transfer the risk to a third party through insurance or outsourcing. The risk still exists, but the financial or operational burden is shared or shifted.

  • When to use: The risk has high financial impact but the organization lacks expertise to manage it
  • Methods: Buy cyber insurance, outsource IT operations to managed service providers

Example: Risk Transfer

A company purchases cyber liability insurance that covers the costs of data breach notification, legal fees, and customer compensation. The risk of a breach still exists, but the financial impact is transferred to the insurance provider.

4. Reduce Consequence

Modify the uses of an asset to reduce the impact should the risk materialize. This does not prevent the incident but limits the damage.

  • When to use: You cannot prevent the threat but can limit its impact
  • Methods: Offsite backups, disaster recovery plans, data segmentation

Example: Reduce Consequence

A company maintains offsite backups of all critical data. If ransomware encrypts their servers, they can restore from backups rather than paying the ransom. The attack still occurs, but the consequence is reduced from Catastrophic to Minor.

5. Reduce Likelihood

Implement suitable controls to make the risk event less likely to occur. This is the most common risk treatment approach.

  • When to use: There are effective controls available at reasonable cost
  • Methods: Firewalls, access controls, encryption, staff training, patching

Example: Reduce Likelihood

A company implements multi-factor authentication (MFA) for all remote access. This makes unauthorized access much less likely even if passwords are compromised, reducing likelihood from "Almost Certain" to "Unlikely."

3. Case Study: Silver Star Mines

About Silver Star Mines

Silver Star Mines is a fictional operation of a global mining company that illustrates how risk assessment and treatment work in a real-world industrial setting.

Company Profile

  • Type: Global mining company with large-scale operations
  • IT Infrastructure: Extensive, including both common business software and industry-specific systems
  • Critical Systems: Some IT systems directly relate to health and safety (SCADA)
  • Network Evolution: Formerly isolated systems are now networked, increasing attack surface

Risk Assessment Approach

  • Silver Star Mines decided on a combined approach to risk assessment
  • The mining industry is at the less risky end of the spectrum compared to financial institutions
  • Management has decided to accept moderate or low risk — only high and extreme risks require treatment

4. Silver Star Mines: Assets

The risk assessment identified the following key IT assets requiring protection:

# Asset Security Properties
1 SCADA nodes and network Reliability and Integrity
2 Stored file and database information Integrity
3 Financial system Availability and Integrity
4 Procurement system Availability and Integrity
5 Maintenance/Production system Availability and Integrity
6 Mail services Availability, Integrity, and Confidentiality

Critical Asset: SCADA Systems

SCADA (Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition) systems are particularly critical because they directly control physical mining operations. Compromise of these systems could lead to equipment damage, environmental incidents, or even loss of life. This is why SCADA reliability and integrity is the highest priority asset.

The following threats and vulnerabilities were identified for each asset:

7. Choosing a Treatment Strategy

Selecting the right treatment alternative depends on several factors. Use the following decision framework:

Risk Treatment Decision Framework

1

Assess Risk Level

Determine if risk is Extreme, High, Medium, or Low

2

Evaluate Options

Consider all five treatment alternatives

3

Cost-Benefit Analysis

Compare treatment cost vs. risk impact

4

Implement & Monitor

Apply chosen treatment and track effectiveness

When to Apply Each Alternative

Treatment Best When... Example
Accept Risk is low or treatment cost exceeds potential loss Minor cosmetic website defacement risk on internal portal
Avoid Risk is too high and cannot be adequately controlled Discontinuing use of an unsupported legacy system
Transfer High financial impact but can be insured or outsourced Purchasing cyber liability insurance for data breaches
Reduce Consequence Cannot prevent the threat but can limit damage Implementing disaster recovery and offsite backups
Reduce Likelihood Effective controls are available at reasonable cost Deploying firewalls, MFA, and intrusion detection

Multiple Treatments

In practice, organizations often apply multiple treatment alternatives to a single risk. For example, Silver Star Mines might reduce the likelihood of SCADA attacks through additional firewalls AND reduce the consequence through isolated safety shutdown systems AND transfer residual risk through insurance.

Summary and Key Takeaways

What We've Learned

  1. Risk Treatment is the process of selecting and implementing measures to modify risk. It follows risk assessment and determines how each identified risk will be handled.
  2. Five Treatment Alternatives exist: Risk Acceptance, Risk Avoidance, Risk Transfer, Reduce Consequence, and Reduce Likelihood. Each has specific use cases and trade-offs.
  3. The treatment decision balances risk level against cost of treatment. Not all risks need active treatment — some can be accepted if the cost of treatment exceeds the potential loss.
  4. The Silver Star Mines case study demonstrates how a real-world organization applies the combined approach to identify assets, threats, vulnerabilities, and build a prioritized risk register.
  5. The Risk Register is the central document for managing risks. It records all identified risks with their analysis and provides a prioritized list for treatment decisions.
  6. Multiple treatments can be applied to a single risk. Organizations often combine strategies for comprehensive risk management.
